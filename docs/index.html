Introduction

In this project we capture camera obscura images with a pinhole over a shoebox.
Camera Obscura is the natural optical phenomenon that occurs when an image is projected through a small hole. What appears on the screen is a reversed and inverted image.

Our Design

All we need are just a box, and pinhole, and a camera.

Box

We cover the five faces inside the cardboard with black paper. The remaining one - which the camera and pinhole faces - is covered with white paper and serves as the screen. We seal the cardboard with black duck tape to prevent the light coming through the margins and corners.

Pinhole

We cut a large hole on the pinhole side of the cardboard and cover it with a black card paper. We've designed a slider slot so that we can insert card paper with pinholes with different diameters. We made pinhole sliders with diameters 0.8mm, 3mm, and 5mm.

Camera

In this project, we use a Sony A7 to capture the images on the screen. The lens attached is a Sony E-mount FE 3.5-5.6/28mm-70mm lens.

Choosing the Pinhole Size

Theoretically, a larger pinhole allows more light to travel in. We need a lower ISO and shorter exposure time in this case.
However, the larger the pinhole, the more blurry our images are.
If we use a smaller pinhole, we might want a higher ISO and longer exposure time to compensate the reduction of light that can be captured.
Although the images are sharper, due to the higher ISO values, our images are more noisy.
The optimal pinhole size for our lens can be calcucated using: $$d = 1.9\cdot\sqrt{f\cdot\lambda} = 1.9\cdot\sqrt{(360mm)\cdot(550\cdot10^{-6}mm)} = 0.845mm$$ The results we got also suggests a 0.8mm pinhole performed the best.
With it we were able to get clear images and capture a great amount of details, despite that the images have a little more noises, which is expected since we use a higher ISO.
